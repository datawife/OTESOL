Language Assessment & Evaluation

Purpose

This section explains how I use assessment to understand and accelerate multilingual learners' growth. Drawing on Module 6, I highlight one assessment tool, its significance, and how I implement it alongside classroom-based monitoring.

Selected Assessment Tool

Tool: WIDA ACCESS (annual summative) with classroom-aligned Can Do Descriptors

Why this tool

  • Comprehensive domains: Assesses listening, speaking, reading, and writing aligned to academic language.
  • Growth and placement: Informs program placement, supports reclassification, and shows longitudinal growth.
  • Instructional alignment: Can Do Descriptors translate scores into concrete expectations for what learners can do at each proficiency level.

Significance in My Learning Program

  • Sets the language growth trajectory: ACCESS levels help me anchor realistic yet ambitious language objectives by content area.
  • Drives differentiation: Domain scores reveal where to intensify scaffolds (e.g., oral rehearsal for students with stronger writing than speaking).
  • Supports collaboration: A shared "data language" for EL specialists, content teachers, and administrators.
  • Equity and advocacy: Ensures appropriate services, accommodations, and avoids premature exit; guides targeted supports for multilingual learners with disabilities.

How I Use It in My TESOL Classroom

Before instruction

  • Profile students: Convert ACCESS domain scores into a one-page learner profile with strengths, stretch areas, and preferred modalities.
  • Set goals: Co-create 1–2 SMART language goals per student (e.g., "Move from short phrases to complete sentences with cause–effect connectors in oral explanations").

During instruction

  • Pair every lesson's language objective to Can Do statements (e.g., Level 2 Speaking: "describe with familiar words + gestures"; Level 4 Writing: "connect ideas with because, so").
  • Group flexibly by domain: Rotate students through stations that target specific domains (listening/speaking lab, reading annotation, writing frames).
  • Scaffold by level: Provide tiered sentence stems/frames matched to proficiency; fade supports as students demonstrate independence.

Formative assessment ecosystem (classroom-based)

Listening

  • 3–4 item picture-based checks
  • Thumbs meter
  • "Draw-what-you-hear"

Speaking

  • 60-second audio responses
  • Simple rubric (comprehensibility, complexity, accuracy)

Reading

  • Annotated short texts
  • Target features highlighted

Writing

  • Micro-writes using target structures (because, however, for example)
  • Single-point rubrics aligned to the lesson's language move (e.g., claim–evidence–reasoning with connectors).
  • Progress trackers: Student-facing charts linking class evidence to Can Do language ("Now I can compare using whereas/however").

After instruction

  • Mini data conferences: Students review samples against their goals and choose a next-step strategy.
  • Reporting: Translate scores and progress into family-friendly snapshots in home languages when possible; highlight assets and next steps.

Monitoring and Reclassification Support

  • Triangulate data: ACCESS + unit assessments + classroom artifacts (audio clips, writing samples, checklists).
  • Watch-list meetings: Short, periodic reviews with content teachers to ensure supports are aligned and effective.
  • Reclassification readiness: Look for consistent performance across domains and content evidence, not just a composite score.

Accommodations and Fairness

  • Ensure testing accommodations align with daily instruction (bilingual glossary, extended time, chunked directions).
  • Teach test genres: Practice the language functions ACCESS expects (describe, explain, argue) within authentic units—avoid "test prep" divorced from content.
  • Trauma- and culture-aware: Normalize anxiety-reduction routines; clarify that assessments inform support, not punishment.

Sample Implementation Snapshot (First 9 Weeks)

Weeks Activities
Week 1–2 Build learner profiles from ACCESS; set domain goals with students.
Week 3–6 Collect two artifacts per domain; give one targeted mini-lesson per week on a language feature connected to content.
Week 7 Mid-cycle conference; update goals and scaffolds.
Week 8–9 Culminating task with dual rubrics (content + language), student reflection attached.

Classroom-Based Tools/Artifacts to Include

  • One-page learner profile template (ACCESS domains → strengths/needs → goals).
  • Single-point rubric for a speaking or writing task tied to a unit.
  • Progress monitoring sheet tracking weekly domain artifacts.
  • Annotated student sample showing growth in a target feature (e.g., adding because/therefore; expanding noun phrases).

If ACCESS isn't used in your district: Alternative Option

Tool: LAS Links (or state ELP assessment) + Classroom Oral Language Inventory

Purpose and use mirror the above: derive domain targets, drive scaffolds, and triangulate with classroom oral/writing samples.

My Commitments

  • Use domain-level data to plan daily language targets and scaffolds.
  • Gather frequent, low-stakes evidence to complement summative scores.
  • Involve students in setting and tracking their language goals.
  • Communicate progress in accessible, asset-oriented ways to families and colleagues.
