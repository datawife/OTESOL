Planning and Instruction

Introduction Reflection

"My progressivist philosophy, centered on 'learning by doing,' comes to life in the planning and instruction phase. Here, the abstract becomes concrete. Successful language learning isn't accidental; it is carefully scaffolded through lessons that are relevant, collaborative, and empowering. My goal is to plan not just for linguistic accuracy, but for communicative confidence. The assignments in Modules 3 and 4 solidified my understanding that a lesson plan is a blueprint for engagement—a flexible guide that prioritizes student interaction over teacher talk, and authentic use over rote practice."

My Guidelines for Successful Language Learning Experiences

Based on my reflections, I've developed these core guidelines for my planning and instruction:

1. Anchor Lessons in Authentic, Goal-Oriented Tasks

Guideline: Every lesson culminates in a primary task that requires students to use the target language to achieve a real-world objective (e.g., plan a trip, negotiate a solution, describe a process to a colleague).

In Practice: This is the "why" of the lesson. Instead of "learn the past tense," the goal becomes "tell a story about a memorable childhood event." This provides immediate context and relevance, making the language learning purposeful for adult learners.

2. Scaffold Rigorously to Bridge the Gap

Guideline: Break down the final task into manageable steps, providing the language, strategies, and support needed for all students to succeed. The mantra "I do, We do, You do" guides this process.

In Practice: I use authentic materials (menus, news clips, brochures) and adapted materials (simplified texts with key vocabulary highlighted). Visual aids, graphic organizers, and sentence frames are non-negotiable tools in my classroom to make challenging input comprehensible.

A simple T-chart used to scaffold a debate on "remote work," helping students organize arguments before speaking.

3. Prioritize Productive Struggle and Collaborative Practice

Guideline: The teacher is a facilitator. The classroom must be a safe space for尝试 (chángshì - trying) and even making mistakes. Learning happens when students grapple with language together.

In Practice: I plan for think-pair-share activities, structured group work, and peer feedback sessions. This reduces reliance on the teacher as the sole source of knowledge and builds a community of learners.

4. Empower Learners with Tools for Autonomy

Guideline: My job is to make myself gradually less necessary. Instruction must include explicit strategy training so students can continue learning outside the classroom.

In Practice: I guide students toward high-quality resources like Duolingo for gamified practice, BBC Learning English or VOA Learning English for listening and current events, and Quizlet for vocabulary building. I often create class-specific Quizlet sets.

We dedicate class time to evaluating useful apps and websites. I provide a "Language Learning Toolkit" handout (and digital list) that categorizes resources by skill: (Listening, Speaking, Pronunciation, Grammar, Vocabulary).

Sample Lesson Sketch: Putting It All Together

Objective:

Students will be able to describe a simple process (e.g., cooking a dish, using an app) using sequencing language (First, Then, Next, After that, Finally) and the imperative mood.

Lead-In (Activate Schema):

Show a short, fun video of someone making a paper airplane without words. Ask "What is he doing? What are the steps?"

Input & Scaffolding:

I Do: Model the language by explaining how to make coffee. Use visuals for each step and write the sequencing words on the board.

We Do: Give students a jumbled series of images showing how to use an ATM. In pairs, they use sentence frames (First, you ___. Then, you ___.) to put the steps in order.

Authentic Task (You Do):

In small groups, students choose a simple process and create a short video or live demonstration explaining it to the class. They must use the target language.

Extension for Autonomy:

I would suggest students try explaining their morning routine to themselves in the mirror using this language, or watch a "how-to" video on YouTube in English and note the sequencing words used.

Conclusion

"Planning through this lens ensures that my classroom is a dynamic workshop where language is used, not just studied. It allows me to bridge the gap between my students' current proficiency and their goals by providing the tools, support, and opportunities for practice they need to become confident, autonomous English users."

Artifacts to Include

  • A lesson plan showing paired objectives and scaffolds (with a student work sample).
  • An anchor chart or sentence frame bank tailored to a unit.
  • A short video clip of a modeled think-aloud or lab demo with captions.
  • A bilingual glossary or text set page.
  • A one-page single-point rubric for a speaking or writing task.

High-Value Resources

  • WIDA Can Do Descriptors and ELD Standards Framework
  • Colorín Colorado lesson ideas and strategy guides
  • Stanford's Understanding Language resources (language demands in content)
  • EL Education curriculum (models and protocols)
  • Newsela or CommonLit with leveled texts and read-aloud
  • Local library databases with multilingual materials

My Planning Commitments

  • Pair every content objective with an explicit language objective and model the target language move.
  • Provide at least two modalities for input and one structured interaction every lesson.
  • Offer an independent practice choice that supports both language and content.
  • Use quick formative data to adjust scaffolds within 24 hours.
  • Honor translanguaging in the planning, drafting, and reflection stages.
